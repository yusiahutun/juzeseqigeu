Carly Simon and Carinthia West Open Up About Love, Heartbreak, and Friendship

February 27, 2024
blog

Carly Simon cautiously descends the spiral staircase of her Martha’s Vineyard home as her four dogs bark and leap enthusiastically. “Remember when I went upstairs two at a time?” she jokes. “No longer!” I commiserate about our rebellious bodies, confiding that I’m finding it harder to get out of a bath. “That’s easily remedied,” she says. “Don’t ever get out!”

I’ve known Carly since the mid-’70s, back when we were nicknamed “the Bookends” by one of our more eloquent rock ’n’ roll friends, the Rolling Stones’ producer Earl McGrath. I was spending time with Mick Jagger and Ronnie Wood at the Plaza hotel in New York, around the corner from Earl’s apartment. One evening while the boys were recording in a studio downtown, Earl thought I might prefer to come to a party he was hosting. Carly and James Taylor were there, and she and I hit it off instantly. As we looked alike in profile and both had long legs, which we liked to curl up beneath ourselves when gossiping on a sofa, we soon had our moniker.

.

Carinthia West with Hercules Bellville and Carly Simon, circa 1980

One of the funniest times I had with Carly occurred on her sofa when I arrived from England jet-lagged, and she told me why she had written “You’re So Vain” and who it was about, only to discover that I was fast asleep and had missed every word. Fortunately she later divulged that the middle stanza was about Warren Beatty, an affair recounted hilariously in her best-selling 2015 memoir, Boys in the Trees. After a late-night tryst, Warren rushes off at 5 a.m. claiming he is working on his latest film. Carly goes to visit her therapist for a morning session, and in the middle of discussing Warren, the therapist turns pale and tells her, “You are not the first patient of the day who spent the night with Warren Beatty last night.”

Paul R Benoit/AP/Shutterstock

Simon with Warren Beatty, 1984

Settling down once again on this very couch in her light-filled living room, with her own still-life watercolors of pears on the mantel while a crackly wood fire burns below, we get to talking about her Carnegie Hall tribute concert, which will raise funds for music education programs for underprivileged children. The vaunted music venue holds a very special place in Carly’s heart. “I have a wonderful history with Carnegie Hall,” she says.“I opened for Cat Stevens there in 1971. I met James there that same year when I went to his dressing room to say hello and he invited himself to my home for a late-night lunch that expanded into a life together.” This fateful coup de foudre is described in Boys in the Trees as they progressed through marriage, two children, hugely popular concert duets like “Mockingbird,” and, ultimately, a very public divorce.

Richard E. Aaron

Simon and James Taylor, 1978

Carly winces and shifts her body on the sofa as her affectionate Cavapoo, Aja, jumps onto her legs. She’s been dealing with a broken hip as well as two painful knee joints. “Both of my knees need to be replaced with pearls!” she says. “They’ll soon have an app where you’ll have a choice of knee shapes and you’d be able to change their color or the song you’d like them to sing.” Like “Haven’t Got Time for the Pain”? I ask. “Well, my real pain is emotional and often centered around James,” she replies.

I am reminded of a line in her memoir of their Carnegie Hall meeting: “Once I laid eyes on James, a sense of possession overtook me, as if a dog fence of little white flags had sprung out of the ground around him. The women on the periphery knew just how far back they must stay.” Perhaps a secret of the success of our friendship is that we’ve never crossed the line into the other’s more serious relationships, especially with James, whom I knew but kept a careful distance from. “Yes, there is a kind of territorial clutch that happens around my heart,” she says. “But you and I have a special bond. I used to think you couldn’t make friends after you were 30, and I met you when I was about 31. I always felt you were the taller, more beautiful, younger me!” Notwithstanding the outrageously flattering compliment, it is true that over the years we’ve sometimes been mistaken for each other; the photographer and art collector Jean Pigozzi once published a photo of my legs with a caption saying they belonged to Carly.

There is a kind of territorial clutch that happens around my heart.
— Carly Simon

We’ve always had that kind of best-girlfriend relationship. There were late nights with her playing piano or guitar. There were secret discussions about our great loves and various entanglements. We never argued and only once fancied the same man, a fatally seductive and successful screenwriter, albeit at different times. “Remember when you started dating him a year after me and you found the ballet shoes in his kitchen bin?” Carly laughs. “I’d told you he was a bit of a wolf and warned you off him!” She was passionate in my defense when I was dumped by a director boyfriend for a glamorous actress, muttering “bastard!” when she next saw him. And I was never afraid to tell her what I thought.

Although I didn’t know her then, I could never quite understand Carly’s early passion for the celebrated English author Willie Donaldson. She met him in 1965 on a trip to England and fell in love. He left the actress Sarah Miles for her, saying at the time, “[She’s] the answer to any sane man’s prayers: funny, quick, erotic, extravagantly talented,” but soon jilted Carly and went back to Miles. Carly forgave him and sent me to meet him some 20 years later. He clearly still held a torch for Carly, but though I was amused by him I reported back to her, rather disparagingly, that I couldn’t understand her fascination for a man who wore scruffy brown jackets with those hideous patches at the elbows. She is still incredulous. “That’s so interesting that you didn’t find Willie attractive!” she exclaims.

Both of us got married again, she to the writer Jim Hart, and I to a man who swept me off to the English countryside. More recently she met Richard Koehler, a surgeon who also loves to sail. “Richard is such a romantic!” she says. “His mother wrote romance novels for Harlequin under several different noms de plume. He has her energy and gets done in five minutes what would take me five weeks.” (Later, when I ask Richard if, after 13 years, he and Carly might one day marry, he replies, “We’ve both been married twice before, so it’s either going to be third time’s a charm or three strikes and you’re out!”)

Joe Corrigan

Simon with her partner, Richard Koehler, 2010

“You have a choice when you write a book about yourself as to how much is going to be true and how much is exaggerated or left out,” Carly says. “It’s the conflict between vulnerability and mystery. And wanting to show one’s vulnerability is not quite as sexy as being mysterious. Yet what I really have to offer is my truth.” I comment that I had read an article on rock memoirs that praised the books of Keith Richards and Elton John while noting that the penultimate “superstar catch” for a book publisher would be Mick or Warren, as neither has been keen to divulge his secrets so far, and Mick once famously returned the advance he’d received to write his autobiography. “The problem is that each person who writes their memoir has to outshock the last person, so therefore the bar becomes higher for you to put in interesting things, memorable things, slimy, sleazy, possible lies,” she says. “Who knows? If I write another and veer off wildly from the truth of my life, I might as well make it a novel.”

Lynn Goldsmith

Mick Jagger, circa 1981

Earlier in the day, Carly played me some outtakes from the 1972 studio session, only recently surfaced, in which Mick sang backup on “You’re So Vain.” It’s soothing to hear the easy banter between them as they croon a few bars of a country and western song. Later that evening I show her and Richard a clip from an upcoming documentary about my photographic work. Mick features in it, generously having agreed to be interviewed, and she comments on what a gift of friendship that is to me. She’s not sure about her own friendship with Mick. “I hope he’s not mad at me. He asked for no credit on ‘You’re So Vain,’ but I feel I should have given him an honorary prize for a record that may have gone down the tubes otherwise.” Warren, the subject of that second stanza, has been married to Annette Bening for more than two decades, and with Carly so happy with Richard, I confess my own longing (post- divorce) to be in one more loving relationship before I hang up my dating shoes. “You must get on Mates or one of those Web sites,” she says, and brightens enthusiastically at the thought of a bit of amateur matchmaking. “Or I could pretend to be you!”
