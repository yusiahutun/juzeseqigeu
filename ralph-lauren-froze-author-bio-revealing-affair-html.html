Ralph Lauren froze out author of his bio for revealing affair

August 10, 2024
blog

Ralph Lauren froze out the author who wrote an unflattering biography about him and left magazine editors so intimidated they did not hire him.

The fashion designer 'hated' Michael Gross's 2003 biography, Genuine Authentic: The Real Life of Ralph Lauren and did not speak to the writer for 15 years - between 2000 and 2015.

Gross was rendered 'persona non grata' in Lauren's world and magazine editors shied away from commissioning him because they feared they would lose Lauren's lucrative advertisements.

But Gross has the last word now as his sizzling book is set to be republished with a new 'afterword' that explains the reason for the animus between the famed American designer and the writer of New York Times bestsellers, Model: The Ugly Business of Beautiful Women, and 740 Park: The Story of the World's Richest Apartment Building.

Gross reveals how one editor told him to 'find something else to do' and how a job he had lined up 'vaporized' after a Ralph Lauren executive said they might lose advertising.

Several sources who were quoted in Genuine Authentic were 'upbraided' by Lauren's son David, Gross claims. 

Ralph Lauren had a 15-year feud with the author of his biography after the book exposed lurid details of his personal life as well as how demanding he was as a boss

Michael Gross wrote Genuine Authentic: The Real Life of Ralph Lauren as an 'unauthorized' biography after he and the designer failed to agree on the terms for the book 

It isn't hard to see why Lauren was fuming.

Lauren originally asked Gross to write the biography with him but when they couldn't agree on the terms, Gross wrote it anyway as an unauthorized version.

Besides chronicling Lauren's rise from a working class Jewish kid in Brooklyn to an American fashion icon, Genuine Authentic claimed that Lauren had a 'terrible dark side' that was at odds with his clean cut public persona.

The book also claimed that in the late 1980s Lauren, who is married, had a fling with Kim Nye, a model who at the time was 27 and the face of the designer's Safari fragrance.

Gross wrote that by 1991 Lauren was dancing naked around a Caribbean villa on a photo shoot with her, at one point turning to her and saying loudly: 'I look at you and I see myself and that's why I love you'.

He also claimed that Lauren's aristocratic-sounding name is pure invention and that he actually descended from East European Jewish immigrants and was born Ralph Lifshitz in October 1939 in a working-class home.

His new name actually came to be when he asked his friends on his 18th birthday if he should change his last name to Lauren or London - all of them voted for Lauren.

Gross wrote that Lauren married his wife Ricky in 1964, a dance teacher whose parents were Austrian because he 'liked the way she looked'.

The book said: 'She was a stunning blonde with sharp features, pale blue eyes and a full figure on a slight frame. She would be a wonderful model for his new women's line'.

In 1987, Lauren nearly died from a brain tumor and at age 50 he had a midlife crisis which led him to cheat on his wife, Gross claimed. 

He began an affair with a British fashion editor who one former aide said was so English that she 'personified peaches and cream'.

Lauren married wife Ricky Low-Beer (pictured with son David) in 1964 because he 'liked the way she looked,' according to Gross

The biography exposed his alleged affair with model Kim Nye (pictured in 1991). He ended the relationship after his wife gave him an ultimatum, Gross claims 

After that he began his 'messily public' affair with Nye which ended when, staffers believe, Ricky issued him an ultimatum - get rid of her or I'm divorcing you.

Lauren is said to have told one employee: 'I don't think Kim is worth trading Ricky for,' but others swore he was still in love with Nye.

In another embarrassing claim, Gross wrote that Lauren, who is 5ft 6in tall, even tried to flirt with Princess Diana but was turned down when she refused to dance with him at a gala, joking: 'He'd only come up to my breasts'.

In the new afterword to the book, Gross says that he 'heard through the grapevine how much he [Lauren] hated' the book, not least because he specifically wanted the affair kept out.

While working as a columnist for the New York Daily News in the 2000s, Gross said he had encounters 'best described as uncomfortable' with one of Lauren's brothers and one of his sons.

Gross writes: 'The fact was, people in Polo's employ had, at times, made it clear I was persona non grata in the Polosphere.

'Glossy magazine editors who'd once given me assignments no longer returned my calls. "You don't get it, do you?" one of them told me. "Find something else to do." Lauren was one of the world's top-spending advertisers and life was too short to risk his ad buys on me'.

Gross wrote that Lauren, who is 5ft 6in tall, even tried to flirt with Princess Diana (pictured next to Anna Wintour) but was turned down when she refused to dance with him at a gala, joking: 'He'd only come up to my breasts'

Gross says that he did not think anyone in Lauren's orbit issued direct  threats - but they didn't really have to.

Due to declining sales editors 'had no choice but to internalize what Ralph Lauren might want - and he'd long made it clear that he expected fealty'.

The humiliation was complete in 2015 when one of Gross's friends had his reservation at The Polo Bar, Lauren's third restaurant in New York, was canceled because he had planned to invite the author along as his guest.

Gross wrote a blog called 'Banned at The Polo Bar? Oy vey!' and the next morning he got a call from Lauren himself. 

The two men spoke for 10 minutes, Gross writes, and Lauren said his social media team had flagged his blog.

Gross writes: 'He wanted to know what inspired it and I told him.

'Then he informed me how I'd hurt him with some of what I'd written about his private life in this book.

'When I heard a few days later that he'd told one of his top executives about the call, it seemed to me that while we'd never be 'friends' again—not that we ever really were—ill feelings might finally be put behind us'.

Gross claims that he never misled Lauren about his intentions with the book and he believes that despite his anger, he 'must have respected that'.

Gross said his friends had a reservation at Lauren's NYC restaurant The Polo Bar (pictured) but it was canceled because he invited the author along as his guest

Fifteen years may be a long time to hold a grudge, but in Genuine Authentic, Gross spoke to dozens of ex-employees of Lauren and the picture they painted was a controlling and mean boss.

Gross wrote that in the late 1990s Lauren thought that Harper's Bazaar editor Liz Tilberis was out to get him so he pulled all of his advertising from her magazine.

Lauren was unmoved by the fact she was suffering from Ovarian cancer - she died in 1999 - and waged a 'secret war against her', Gross wrote.

As Gross described it: 'Away from the cameras Lauren's blinding smile gave way to a nasty temper and terrible rages'.

Gross quoted one acquaintance of Lauren as saying: 'Anyone who gets close to him is left reeling by the assaults on his self-esteem, his need to diminish people and make them feel helpless, infantile and inept in his presence.

'I always wondered what huge hurt he'd suffered in his childhood to make him act that way'.

Gross summed up Lauren by saying that the things which made him a success like obsessiveness and control were 'negative when viewed on the human level'.

In the new afterword Gross strikes a more conciliatory tone and says that designer Michael Kors is a 'relative whippersnapper' next to Lauren.

He says that while others in the fashion business got rich, nobody approached Lauren's 'fame, wealth, or position of influence'.
