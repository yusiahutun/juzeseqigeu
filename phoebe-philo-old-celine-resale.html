Phoebe Philo era Celine is more popular than ever on resale

January 26, 2024
blog

In two month's time, Phoebe Philo will unveil the highly-anticipated debut collection from her eponymous brand – five years after leaving Celine, the Parisian fashion house she transformed into the most-coveted brand in the world. A lot has changed in that half-decade, in both the world at large and the world of fashion. A lot. (Even Celine lost its acute accent). But the level of passion for Philo-era Céline is not one of them.

When she departed, Philophiles the world over pledged their allegiance to ‘Old Céline’ – the affectionate moniker that has come to denote her decade-long tenure at the brand – some by following fan accounts set up on Instagram, many by snapping up anything they could with Philo’s design handwriting on it before it was too late. Sales of secondhand Old Céline surged – and never stopped. In fact, since Philo announced that her new brand would launch this September, searches for 'Old Céline' have surged 70 per cent on Ebay. It's a phenomenon completely at odds with the churn of the fashion cycle; the old coveted over the new.

Stephane Cardinale - Corbis//Getty Images

But why? Undoubtedly, her ability to design as a woman for women carries heft. To create pieces that gave practicality and beauty equal billing, pieces that were refreshingly devoid of the male gaze. “Fashion was very different before she started at Celine,” says Sabrina Marshall, co-founder of luxury resale site Re-See, on which Old Céline ranks in the top three most-wanted brands. “She changed the fashion landscape. She knew how to balance making us dream with the reality of how a modern woman really dresses. She'll go down in history, the same way that Yves Saint Laurent did for starting ready-to-wear and liberating women.”

Erica Wright, founder of fashion sourcing app Sourcewhere, agrees: “The collections were always inspiring but with a sense of practicality, especially at a time when there were fewer brands focusing on designing pieces that could be worn from the school run to meetings and then to a dinner date.” At Sourcewhere, it is the most requested brand for past-season finds.

Stephane Cardinale - Corbis//Getty Images

So who are the women fuelling the red-hot secondhand circuit? Perhaps predictably, primarily professional women between 30 and 60. One such customer is Frédérique Gilain-Huneeus, a chief legal officer at an Asia-based hedge fund, who started building her collection in real time and then continued via the resale market when Philo left in 2018. Though she’s never counted – “when you love, you don’t count, do you?” – she thinks she owns somewhere in the region of 300 Old Céline pieces: “Perhaps more of the accessories, bags and shoes.” Her most recent purchase was a navy open-back ribbed dress from the autumn/winter 2015 collection.

What’s more interesting is the lower end of the demographic, which is made up of women who didn’t – or couldn’t – buy it the first time round, like Yana McKillop, a stylist and fashion editor. “I was making the first steps in my career when Phoebe started at Celine and owning something from it was an unachievable dream at that stage,” she explains, “but it embodied a vision of a woman I wanted to be.” She has since built up a substantial personal archive and occasionally sources and sells Old Céline too via @yanasarchive.

That the designs still resonate as clearly now as they did when they were fresh off the catwalk is perhaps the ultimate testament to Old Céline’s ability to withstand the test of time. But if the cost was prohibitive when it was new, it’s often even more so now. This is rare – we think of resale as an avenue to get things at a more affordable price, but according to Marshall, Old Céline has the potential to hit Hermès bag levels when it comes to investability (Hermès bags are famously one of the only luxury items on the market that appreciate in value). Aside from the enduring aesthetic appeal, the quality of Old Céline is such that pieces have the potential to have an incredibly long lifespan, explains Marshall.

At Sourcewhere, items often outstrip their original retail price, with classic bags – including the Trapeze and Cabas – and pull-on sneakers known to fetch high prices. But it is iconic campaign and runway items that consistently outstrip their former RRP – like the Madame boots (spring/summer 2018) and Edge sunglasses (seen on Daria Werbowy in the SS13 campaign).

Antonio de Moraes Barros Filho//Getty Images

Across the board, the most-coveted ‘unicorn’ piece appears to be the Foulard shirt from the spring/summer 2011 collection, famously worn on-stage by Kanye West at Coachella. Sourcers continually find it hard to track down and it very rarely appears on resale sites. If it does come up for sale, the price tag is huge (one has previously sold on peer-to-peer marketplace Grailed.com for over $5,000) and it sells instantly. For Gilain-Huneeus it’s the piece she most regrets not buying first time round. “The price for this piece on the secondhand market is unreasonable in my opinion – I have the matching trousers though and I am very happy with them!” With a finite amount of Old Céline in the world, it’s the scarcity principle in action.

Michel Dufour//Getty Images

The Foulard shirt on the catwalk

Martina Lohoff is one such sourcer who has been challenged to find the Foulard shirt. A design technology and economics instructor from Dortmund, she started collecting Celine in 2013 and set up The Old Céline Archive in 2020 as a platform to source and sell the rarest pieces via Instagram. She now has 30,000 dedicated followers tracking her discoveries and last year joined forces with Re-See to hold an exclusive sale of 200 archive pieces during Paris Fashion Week, which fashion insiders were “fighting to come to”, according to Marshall. She’s most regularly tasked with finding the Yves Klein ‘Anthropometrie’ dress and another with spider web bra detailing, both from spring/summer 2017, and a blanket from Philo’s final collection, created in collaboration with graphic designer Peter Miles. But even she hasn’t been able to track down a porcelain-detail choker from the cruise 2016 collection… yet: “I was told it was only shown in the lookbook and never made it into the boutiques.”

But what of the thrill, or lack thereof, of buying such prized items from behind a screen as opposed to a polished store? “As Céline was never sold online when it was under Phoebe, it was always a bit of a sacred ritual to go to the store and see and feel the pieces,” says Yana McKillop. “(But) I would say there’s definitely more thrill when you hunt down that piece you’ve been looking for for years in some cases.”

What’s most clear is that the women buying Old Céline are buying it to be worn – from statement pieces to classic, everyday knitwear – not just to simply own it. “I don’t envision my ‘collection’ as one to be kept in a safe; I wear all of my pieces, every day; they are a part of me somehow,” says Frédérique Gilain-Huneeus. After all, it was the real-life wearability of Old Céline that resonated so deeply with women in the first place – and if the bubbling trend for ‘quiet luxury’ is anything to go by, it seems Philo’s design sensibilities are coming very much back into focus, just in time for her grand return.
