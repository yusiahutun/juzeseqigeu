'We'll never forget, we'll never forget, we'll never forget': The heart-wrenching words of the broken father who has become the symbol of a nation's grief

Overwhelmed by emotion to the point of collapse, he is the broken father who has come to symbolise a nation's grief.

Robert Peraza lost his son on 9/11 after he was trapped on the 104th floor of the North Tower, just above the gaping hole left in the building by the impact of American Airlines flight 11.

Although ten years have passed since his son Rob came down with the towers, Mr Peraza showed just how strong the feeling of loss still is for those who lost a loved one that day.

Grief: Robert Peraza, who lost his son Robert David Peraza, pauses at his son's name at the North Pool of the 9/11 Memorial

As the 9/11 Memorial and Museum was opened today to families of victims, Mr Peraza touched his son's name, fell to his knees and lowered his head.

The same man later gathered the courage to rise the stairs to the podium where family members read the names of loved ones who perished during the terror attacks.

Wearing a t-shirt bearing his son's face, Mr Peraza announced that he would 'never forget' his son.

'Rob, we love you,' he said. 'We are with you all the time and we'll never forget, we'll never forget, we'll never forget.'

Content: Rob wrote his parents a letter a month before the attacks describing how happy he was in New York

Mr Peraza said he did not have to look hard for his son's name on the memorial - he was somehow just drawn to it.

'I just began to walk, and I found it,' he said.

'It was, to me, very emotional to find the name. I knew it was in the north pool. It’s very moving.'

He added: 'All I am doing here is to honour his memory. The issue isn’t about me.'

Mr Peraza told the New York Post that his family had worked tirelessly to keep Rob's memory alive, raising almost $250,000 to fund a scholarship in his son's name at St. Bonaventure University, where he studied.

Rob died aged 30 on 9/11, a bond trader at Cantor Fitzgerald who was trapped above the smash and never returned home afterwards.

He was a keen sportsman, who played rugby for his university team and ran marathons. Just a month before the attacks he wrote his parents a four-page letter describing how happy he was with his life in New York and how he was considering proposing to his long-term girlfriend.

After the ceremony, Mr Peraza, who also worked in finance, said: 'He wrote to say how wonderful his life was.

'He was about to be promoted. He was dating a girl from St. Bonaventure. They were buying furniture and talking about marriage.'

Mr Peraza said it was important the family stayed strong and tried to move on from the tragedy because Rob would not have wanted them to have 'wilted'.

'Rob was a very joyful person,' he added. 'He was a people person. He was the kind of guy if he had $20 in his pocket and a friend came and was in need he would give that person the money.

'My wife, Thursday, found a way to print a picture of Rob when he was the best man for his brother's wedding. He was in a tuxedo. It was a very, very nice picture. She had the picture right up by the phone.

Sporty: Rob was a keen sportsman and played rugby competitively at university

'I kept passing by the phone and I told her: "Please let's put the picture down",' Mr Peraza told WCPO.

He added of the terrorists who killed his son: 'I'm not going to let those guys ruin my life. I'm going to live a life to work against what they're trying to do.'

Ron had gained an undergraduate degree and MBA from St. Bonaventure University, where he met the girl he was still dating when he died, Megan Cressy.

Sometime in the almost two hours between the plane hitting and the North Tower going down, Rob perished.

We has outlived by his parents, Robert and Suzanne, as well as a brother, Neil.

Since his passing his family have strived to keep his legacy alive.

Ten years later the Peraza family now has two scholarships in their son's name. One is with the New York state school system and the second is a Rugby scholarship with St. Bonaventure.

An annual Rugby Alumni weekend at St. Bonventure has also been renamed in his honour and a golf tournament named after him is run once a year.

'Rob played Rugby and his friends from St. Bonaventure have been helping out with the Rugby scholarship. With the scholarship we have raised about $250,000 and have put two kids through college with a partial scholarship and we plan to do more in the future,' Mr Peraza said.

Overwhelmed: Robert takes a moment to think about his son at the memorial

Honour: Mr Peraza was distraught today and wore a t-shirt with his son's face on with pride

Rob's father also recounted the terrible days after 9/11, when they learned their son must have perished.

'We left on the 12th at noon time for New York City,' he said. 'We stayed with my wife's brother. The next day we went to Manhattan.

'The toughest part in the whole process was about Friday morning the police had set up an office in the armoury in Manhattan and they called the parents to a room and they were going through a list of people that they had and the list was basically a list of people who had not gone to work that day' - Rob was not on the list.

Even before his father's striking gesture today, Rob's parents have previously spoken out.

Soon after his death his father said: 'We're trying to honour him so his life will have a meaning. We're doing some good deeds, and some scholarships in his name, so that other people can be able to benefit from his life.'

Just days after the attacks, Rob's mother, Suzanne Peraza, said: 'He was born in 1971 when they first started building the World Trade Center.'

'Now, 30 years later, in 2001, that's his coffin. That's what's been going through my mind.'
